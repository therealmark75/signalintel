# The Arrival

The harbour town never slept, not really. It only dimmed, the way a lamp dims when someone turns the wick down without the courage to put it out. Mara had learned that in her first week, lying awake in the narrow room above the chandlery, listening to the slow argument of rope and tide.

She had come north for work and found, instead, a question. The crates that arrived each Thursday on the *Kestrel* were addressed to a company that did not appear in any register she could find. They were heavy. They did not rattle. And the men who carried them up from the quay never spoke.

> "You ask too much for someone who wants to keep her position," the harbourmaster told her, not unkindly.

She wrote it down anyway, because writing things down was the only way she knew to make the world hold still long enough to be understood.

## A Note on the Ledgers

The ledgers were the first thing she noticed and the last thing anyone wanted her to read. Three columns: date, weight, destination. The destination column was always the same single word, and the word was not a place.

That night she copied four pages by candlelight, her hand cramping, the sea breathing against the pilings below. When she finished she blew out the candle and lay in the dark, certain of two things. The first was that she had crossed a line she could not uncross.

The second she would not admit to herself for another three weeks.

---

Morning came grey and certain. Mara folded the copied pages into the lining of her coat, went down to the chandlery, and began, as she did every day, to pretend that nothing had changed.
